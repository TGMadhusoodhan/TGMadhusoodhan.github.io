Two AI Systems Are Fighting Over Your Hospital Bill. Neither One Is on Your Side.

How a $140 billion administrative arms race became the most consequential use of AI in American healthcare, and what a different model might look like.

Madhusoodhan T.G. · ML Engineer, AI/Healthcare Systems · June 2025

The story starts in a car

A woman gives birth in a car, running red lights through Orlando at midnight.

Her son survives 56 days in the NICU. Real care. Real machines keeping a real baby alive.

Then the bill arrives. $550,000. With a payment plan of $45,843 a month.

She did not create that number. Two AI systems fighting over her son's chart created it.

The bill was eventually corrected. A journalist started asking questions, actual people reviewed the numbers, and the family was saved close to half a million dollars. But that is not the comforting part of the story. That is the damning part. A system should not require a reporter to function correctly. For every Bisi Bennett whose case reaches a journalist, there are thousands of families who quietly pay, or quietly stop fighting, or simply disengage from a healthcare system they no longer trust.

This story, reported in STAT News by the chief medical officer of Machinify, is almost less about the numbers and more about what produced them.

What is actually happening

Most people assume hospital bills are expensive because healthcare is expensive. That is partly true. But there is another reason, one that has almost nothing to do with the care itself.

The US hospital payment system works like this: when you are discharged, the hospital does not hand you an itemized receipt. It translates everything that happened to you into codes. ICD-10 codes. Each diagnosis, each procedure, each complication: it is all a code. And each code pays a different amount.

“Sepsis” pays more than “infection.” “Severe sepsis” pays more than regular sepsis. “Septic shock” pays even more. The clinical difference between these is real. The coding difference is where things get interesting.

Hospitals have entire departments (called revenue cycle management) whose only job is to look at your chart and find the highest-paying code that can technically be justified. This is legal. Every hospital does it.

The mechanism has grown more specific. Hospitals now deploy what is called ambient listening AI: software that records every physician-patient conversation and automatically populates the electronic health record with secondary diagnoses. The physician may never review what was added. The AI hears a clinical conversation, identifies conditions that justify higher billing codes, and inserts them into the chart. No human deliberately writes anything false. The inflation happens automatically, in the background, at scale.

Insurance companies receive these bills and push back. They have their own teams reviewing claims, denying them, requesting more documentation, and delaying payment. Also legal. Also universal.

This back-and-forth fight happens on every single claim, across thousands of hospitals and millions of patients. And it costs $140 billion a year. Not in care. Just in the arguing.

Wait, aren't insurance companies the good guys here?

This is the most interesting question. And the honest answer is: neither side is the villain. Both sides are rational actors in a broken system.

The insurer pushing back on a sepsis code? Sometimes they are right. Sometimes the hospital inflated a code and the insurer correctly rejected it. In that moment, the insurer's financial interest happened to align with the right outcome.

But the insurer's AI is not asking “is this claim legitimate?” It is asking “can we pay less?” Those are not the same question. It pushes back on everything, legitimate and illegitimate claims alike, because statistically, a percentage of hospitals will just accept less rather than fight.

The real problem: nobody in this system has the patient's interest as their objective function. The hospital's AI is not asking “what is fair for this patient?” The insurer's AI is not asking it either. Their chart is the battlefield. Their bill is the outcome.

The government's own auditors flagged a single Medicare diagnosis in 2019 that generated $7.4 billion across 581,000 patients, and diplomatically noted the length of stay “didn't quite match the severity of the diagnoses.”

Nobody is getting sicker or healthier. The bots are just getting better at arguing.

This is a game theory problem

Strip away the medical complexity and what you have is an adversarial game between two players with misaligned incentives.

  • Hospital: “How do we maximize what we get paid?”
  • Insurer: “How do we minimize what we pay out?”
  • Patient: standing in the middle holding a $550,000 bill.

Both sides built AI to play their respective games more efficiently. More sophisticated AI means a more expensive war. The $140 billion is not waste in the careless sense. It is the cost of running two opposed war machines on the same patient record.

And here is the twist: the insurer is currently winning. Medicare Advantage denials jumped 55.7% between 2022 and 2023 alone. Only 14% of hospitals have deployed AI to fight back. As one industry expert put it, the volume has created a situation that is “not a human-addressable problem anymore at this scale.”

This arms race ends one of three ways: mutual disarmament, one side winning, or the game changing entirely.

What a better system looks like

Companies like Machinify are thinking about the third option: changing the game.

The current flow is: patient discharged, hospital inflates codes, bill sent to insurer, insurer AI pushes back, both sides fight for months, patient gets a terrifying bill.

The proposed alternative is one neutral AI that adjudicates the claim at the moment of discharge, grounded in clinical reality rather than coded claims. The insight is deceptively simple: clinical facts are harder to game than codes. Ambient listening AI can auto-populate a chart with diagnoses, but it cannot fabricate that blood cultures were ordered or that vasopressors were dispensed. Objective records are the immune system of the whole design.

The hard part is not the technology

The architecture is solvable. The business problem is harder.

Both sides have to simultaneously disarm for this to work. The hospital that is winning some billing fights has no individual incentive to give up their AI weapon. The insurer profiting from systematic denial has no individual incentive to pay more accurately.

There are really only three realistic adoption paths:

  • Employers forcing it. Large self-insured employers like Amazon, Walmart, and GM write the healthcare check directly. If they demand their hospital networks use neutral adjudication, hospitals comply or lose those contracts.
  • Government mandating it. CMS requiring neutral adjudication for Medicare and Medicaid claims would accelerate everything. But political will moves slowly.
  • Proving it in narrow domains. Start with one claim type where both sides lose the most in fighting. NICU bills are a good example. Show that neutral adjudication settles those faster, cheaper, and more accurately. Build trust. Expand.

The technical solution is ready to be built. The economic and regulatory conditions that would let it scale are still catching up.

Where this goes

The arms race framing is right. But here is what it misses: the ultimate cost of this war is paid by patients who had nothing to do with starting it.

The damage does not stop at the bill. Patients who receive a $550,000 installment plan tend to disengage quietly. They skip follow-up visits. They do not fill prescriptions. They decide the system is not worth fighting and disappear. When they reappear, their conditions have often progressed. The financial side effects of care have become clinical ones.

That woman running red lights through Orlando at midnight was not thinking about ICD codes. She was thinking about her son surviving. And he did survive. Fifty-six days in the NICU, which is genuinely heroic medicine.

The $550,000 bill is what happened when two AI systems got done arguing over the charts of that heroic medicine. Neither one was asking whether the amount was fair. Neither one knew her name.

Bisi Bennett's family got their story told. Most families do not. Building a system that settles what care costs before the patient ever sees a bill is not just a technical problem. It is what healthcare AI should actually be for.
